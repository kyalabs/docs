Every study returns one artifact: a **report package**. Not a slide someone wrote about a study — the study itself, in a form you can read in five minutes or audit line by line.

One artifact does two jobs. The headline is for the room where the decision gets made; the receipts are for the analyst who has to stand behind it. The executive reading the verdict and the engineer checking the math are reading the same package.

A package has three layers, bound in one direction: the read is computed from the data, the data from the evidence — so the headline can never assert a result the counts don't support.

## The question a study answers

When a shopper asks an AI about your category — "what's the best mid-size SUV," "how should I take payments online" — an agent shops the live web and lands on a recommendation. That answer is a surface you compete on, and most teams have never seen it. A study runs that act at scale and brings you the read:

- **Where do you land** when an agent answers your category's question?
- **Which agents** surface you, which pass you over, and which never reach you at all?
- **What did they see** — your live product pages, or only what a search index cited about you?
- **How does that move** quarter over quarter, before and after a launch, against a competitor?

The deliverable is the answer to those questions, with the receipts attached.

## What it is not

A number you can't defend in a board meeting is a liability, not an asset. So every package states its own limits on its face. A controlled-mission study of real AI shopping surfaces is:

- **not** a market-share measurement;
- **not** a forecast of human demand;
- **not** a public benchmark or league table;
- **not** a ranking of model providers.

This claim boundary is a required field on every report, rendered on every surface of the artifact — not a disclaimer someone remembers to add. [The Honesty Layer](/evidence/honesty-layer) is how that stays true mechanically.

And it is not a visibility dashboard that scrapes AI answers and charts a mention count. It is a research instrument: every number opens to the trip, the source, and the receipt behind it, on a versioned panel built for quarter-over-quarter comparison. A trend you can audit, not a score you have to trust.
