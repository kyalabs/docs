A ShoppiPanel study is a population of *trips*. Each trip is one AI shopping agent, asked your category's question once, doing what it actually does: reaching the web, reading or being cited, deciding, and answering. The report you read is a projection of those trips. This page follows a single trip from the moment it sets out to the moment its evidence lands in a receipt you can open — and shows why every number in the report can be walked back to exactly the trips that produced it.

The promise is narrow and testable: **no claim in the report floats.** Every recommendation, every count, every "this is what the agents did" carries at least one reference to a specific piece of captured evidence, and that evidence is retrievable.

## What one trip records

When a trip runs, three things about it are written down as they happen — not reconstructed afterward:

<CardGroup cols={3}>
  <Card title="The transcript">
    The full turn-by-turn record of what the agent was asked, what it reasoned, and what it said. The agent's own words, captured at the time, with credential and secret-token patterns scrubbed.
  </Card>
  <Card title="The journey">
    Which sources the trip actually touched — the pages a browsing agent fetched, or the sources a search-native agent (Perplexity Sonar) was given as citations. Each source is recorded with where it came from.
  </Card>
  <Card title="The access posture">
    For a browsing agent, whether it *reached* a page and read it, or was *access-limited* on a page that turns away automated traffic. Recorded per attempt, as a graded outcome — never collapsed to a single absolute word.
  </Card>
</CardGroup>

A trip also carries the plain facts you would expect: which model family stood in for it (GPT, Gemini, Claude, or Perplexity Sonar), which lane it ran (browse or search-native), and whether it completed with enough evidence to count. Those travel with the trip as a stamped record, so a trip is never an unanchored anecdote — it is an addressable unit with a provenance.

## From a source the agent touched to a receipt you can open

Every source a trip used becomes one **evidence card** — a small, uniform record that says where the source came from (a page the agent fetched, or a source the search-native model cited), what was on it, and which trip used it. The two lanes produce the same kind of card, so a browse trip and a search-native trip both resolve to the same evidence currency. That shared shape is what carries a single source-backed count across both lanes: browse and search-native trips fold into one denominator, end-to-end today — not the browse lane scored alone with search-native bolted on later. ([Two Lanes](/how-it-works/two-lanes) covers how the two retrieval modes are captured and why they reconcile to one currency.)

This is what makes the report auditable rather than merely confident. In a shipped report package, the evidence ledger is not a summary — it is the cards themselves. Each card names the trip it came from, the source it represents, and its locator (for a browse source, the URL the agent actually opened). It also marks honestly what kind of claim it can support: a card that records *what an agent did* is kept distinct from a card that asserts *a fact about a product*. A count claim and a product-fact claim are never backed by the same undifferentiated blob.

The practical effect for a reader: when the report states a number, you can find the cards behind it, and each card points at the trip and the source it came from. The evidence is the appendix, not a footnote to it.

## Counts before rates

Before the report states any rate or any verdict, it states the counts. A shipped report carries a count ledger that declares, for the study, how many trips were:

<Steps>
  <Step title="Planned">
    The trips the study set out to run.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Completed">
    The trips that ran to an answer.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Access-limited">
    Trips that met a page that turns away automated traffic and so could not contribute — counted *as* access-limited, never silently dropped.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Insufficient">
    Trips that came back without enough evidence to count.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Metric-eligible">
    The trips that actually carry the report's numbers.
  </Step>
</Steps>

A rate with no denominator is a place to hide. "Most agents recommended you" means nothing until you can see that it was, say, a clear majority of the metric-eligible trips — with the planned, access-limited, and insufficient trips all counted and visible. The ledger makes the denominator a first-class fact of the report, so the rate can be checked against it rather than taken on faith.

Every line in that ledger carries its own evidence references back to the trips it summarizes. And the winner a trip reports is the winner the agent itself declared, carried through to the count as a structured entity — not a word guessed back out of its prose afterward. The count is not asserted; it is sourced. That sourced count is what the report's headline is computed from before any sentence is written — the mechanism [The Honesty Layer](/evidence/honesty-layer) lays out in full.

## Access posture, stated honestly

A browsing trip regularly meets pages that don't serve automated traffic. The report never collapses that into a single absolute refusal label. We describe what an agent could and couldn't reach in two states — *reached*, or *access-limited* — rather than using wording that would hide who limited the access and how completely.

<Note>
  The headline stays at *reached / access-limited*. A finer reading of **how** a page was limited — open, partially walled to automated traffic, or closed to it — rides on the per-finding receipt, where the evidence for that reading lives. It is a discipline of observation, recorded as we see it; it is not a promise that any given page can be reached.
</Note>

Where a trip's path to a page was constrained, the report says so plainly, and the access-limited trip is counted *as* access-limited in the ledger rather than converted into a false negative about your brand. A reach gap is the property of the run that hit it, recorded honestly, and never laundered into a fake fact about a site or a fake trend across months. [Two Lanes](/how-it-works/two-lanes) is where the reached / access-limited model and its attribution discipline live in full.

## Retrievable, not just claimed

A receipt that exists only on an engineer's machine is not a receipt. The report's evidence — its readable report, its receipts appendix, and its proof view — is produced as part of the report package and is retrievable today through a supported read path, with no one needing server access to dig it out. The package itself carries a content hash and pointers to its core receipts, so a delivered report can be located and its evidence opened. The evidence is there to be pulled.

Every served surface of the report passes through the same sanitization that keeps internal vocabulary off the buyer face — so the receipt you open is in the buyer's register, not the engine's.

To be precise about what is shipped versus what is being wired:

- **Live today** — the read path that makes the evidence retrievable. Any number in a report can be audited back to its trips and sources, on request.
- **Being wired now** — the polished self-serve walk-back: clicking straight from a headline number, to the trip, to that trip's transcript and the sources it touched, without leaving the product. By design, in progress.

## The chain a number rests on

The trust you place in a number is not a request to trust a narrative. It is a request to trust a chain — every link of which is recorded, addressable, and auditable.

## Short answers

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title='If a report says "19 of 25 agents surfaced my brand," can I see the 19?'>
    Yes. The count is backed by evidence references that resolve to the trips behind it, and each trip's evidence — what it touched, what it said — is in the report's evidence ledger. The number is sourced, not asserted.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="What exactly do you capture per agent?">
    Its transcript (turn-by-turn, with secret-token patterns scrubbed), its journey (the sources it actually used and where they came from), its access posture (reached vs. access-limited), and the plain facts — model family (GPT, Gemini, Claude, or Perplexity Sonar), lane, and whether it counted.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="What happens when an agent couldn't reach my page?">
    The report says *access-limited* and counts that trip honestly in the denominator. A reach gap on one run is never converted into a false fact about your site or a fake trend.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Can a finding exist without evidence behind it?">
    No. Every recommendation, finding, and count carries at least one evidence reference; in a shipped report the evidence ledger is the cards themselves, not a summary of them.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Do I need engineering help to get the receipts?">
    No. The report's readable view, receipts appendix, and proof view are retrievable through a supported read path, with no server access required — that read path is live today. The walk-back from a number to its trips and sources is available on request now; the polished self-serve click-through is being wired.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>
