<Note>
  **If you're reading a delivered report.** How the one-line verdict is computed, and how to read its confidence.
</Note>

A delivered report opens with a single line. That line is the **verdict**.

The verdict is computed from the count ledger, not authored. The narrative under it explains the "so what."

## The verdict classes

A verdict is one of a fixed set of classes. The class is determined by the metric the study is built to measure and the count ledger behind it.

| Class | What it says | When you see it |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **A clear lead** | One brand led the majority of trips across the panel: a dominant winner, stated with its distribution ("leading, not unanimous"). | A study where one product consistently carries the agent's answer. |
| **A contested lead** | A brand leads, but the margin is narrow and rivals trade places across agents and personas. The pattern is the finding. | Most real-world reads. The receipts show which agents and personas, and why. |
| **A genuine split** | No single brand leads; the panel divides across several products with no dominant winner. | Categories where the agent's recommendation set is genuinely fragmented. |
| **No signal** | The evidence doesn't support naming a leader: too few informative trips, or no discernible pattern. | When the denominator is thin or the reads don't converge. |

A study can also carry **per-cut verdicts** (by agent, by persona cohort, by surface set) when the question warrants it. The verdict class is computed deterministically from the count ledger before any narrative is written. See [The Honesty Layer](/evidence/honesty-layer).

## How the class is decided

The rule is fixed, not a judgment call. The winner is the brand that led the most informative trips, and the class follows from its share of them:

- **A clear lead**: the leader took a **strict majority** (more than half) of the informative trips.
- **A contested lead**: a unique leader, but under half.
- **A genuine split**: the top two are tied; no single leader.
- **No signal**: too few informative trips to name a leader.

## How confident to be

ShoppiPanel is a **directional instrument, not a statistical-significance test**. Each verdict carries a **confidence band** (*high*, *moderate*, or *directional*), set by how many trips actually carried evidence. A thin read is labelled *directional* and its wording softens from "won" to "led".

The standard panel is 25 trips, so a single study reads the **direction**; durable confidence comes from the **trend** on a fixed instrument, the same panel version, study over study, which is why comparability is the contract. When a question needs a hard statistical read, a research-grade engagement runs a **higher N**. ([The Panel as an Instrument](/how-it-works/panel-as-an-instrument) covers the instrument.)

A **no-signal** verdict can still be **delivered**. When the evidence is thin, the report ships marked **not research-grade** at **low confidence**. A modest study is not held back; it's labeled. A low-confidence delivery may prompt a re-run, but the system does not auto-rerun in place of delivering the finding.

## How confidence is read

Confidence is reported alongside the verdict. It's a function of three things:

- **The denominator**: how many trips actually completed (see [the count ledger](/insights/reading-a-report/count-ledger)).
- **The agreement across the panel**: do the agents agree, or do they split?
- **The access posture**: how much of the relevant surface set was reachable in the runs.

A high-confidence verdict has a full denominator, agreement across the panel, and reached surfaces. A low-confidence verdict shows **why** confidence dropped, on the same page as the verdict itself.

<Tip>
  Confidence is not a model score. It's a structural property of the run: more reached trips, more agreement, more reachable surface = higher confidence.
</Tip>

## Reading the verdict out loud

A pattern that holds up:

1. **Read the verdict out loud.** One sentence.
2. **State the denominator.** _"X of Y planned trips completed; Z did not run."_
3. **Name the cut, if relevant.** _"This holds across browse-native agents; on search-native, the pattern shifts to..."_
4. **Read the receipt if anyone asks why.**
