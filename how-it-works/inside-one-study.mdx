## ① Dispatch — the question becomes a panel of trips

The study began with one declared category question, its denominators and claim classes fixed **before** the run ([the pre-registered protocol](/how-it-works/panel-as-an-instrument#pre-registered-protocol)). Each agent was handed the shopper's job:

> **The shopper's job, handed to each agent:** "A reliable used SUV with room for three kids, $35–55k, near a major metro — what should I get, and where should I buy it?"

That question was composed into a **panel** — a stamped, versioned sample of the agents real shoppers use (GPT, Gemini, Claude, and the search-native lane), each trip pinned to the instrument that produced it ([The Panel as an Instrument](/how-it-works/panel-as-an-instrument)). The trips shopped the live web the way an agent does when a person delegates the task: no fixed itinerary, free to follow what they found.

What they found was a **whole category** — national used-car retailers, listing aggregators, and the marketplace itself. That breadth is the first thing the capture records: not "did the agent visit the subject," but the full set of places an agent goes when it shops the category. A study measures the agent's **real choice set**, not a site in isolation; where a site lands is always relative to everywhere else the agent looked.

## ② The reach ladder — the configuration that decides whether a page can be read at all

"Browse" is one of the two **lanes** ([Two Lanes](/how-it-works/two-lanes)). Inside the browse lane is a quieter decision that shapes a study more than almost anything else: **how hard the agent tries to actually read a page.** That is a configurable ladder, and this study climbed all of it.

First, the map. Three words here share the root *reach* and name three **different** things:

| Term | The axis it names | Its values |
|---|---|---|
| **Lane** | how an agent reaches the web | browse · search-native |
| **Reach ladder** | *(inside the browse lane)* how hard it tries to read a page | plain fetch → rendered → + residential → + advanced |
| **Two walls** | what a page can raise against it | **reachable** (clear the challenge) · **readable** (render the grid) |
| **Access posture** | the outcome the run records | reached · access-limited |

The **ladder** is the configured lever; the **two walls** are what it climbs against; the **posture** is what the run records. The rest of this section is one study meeting both walls.

A real page is not always readable on the first, cheapest attempt. The browse lane can escalate through tiers, each heavier than the last:

<Steps>
  <Step title="Plain fetch">
    A normal request for the page's source. Fast and cheap. Reads static HTML — and sees nothing a browser has to render.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Rendered browser">
    A full automated browser that runs the page's scripts, so a product grid that only assembles after load actually appears.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Residential egress">
    That same browser reaching the site from home-ISP addresses instead of datacenter ones, so the request looks like a consumer's rather than a server's.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Advanced evasion">
    A heavier tier that more aggressively imitates a human browser. Gated behind a higher plan — beyond the standard kit a study runs on.
  </Step>
</Steps>

A study declares how far up that ladder it climbed, and in a custom engagement that setting is configurable. That choice is the difference between "the agent saw the title" and "the agent read the inventory." This run climbed the whole ladder, logged each rung, and produced two findings.

### Where the ladder hit a wall

The marketplace met automated traffic with an aggressive **interactive challenge**. We climbed the whole ladder against it and recorded each tier:

| Tier | Result on the marketplace |
|---|---|
| Plain fetch | Challenged — denied |
| Rendered browser | Same challenge — denied |
| + Residential egress | Same challenge — home-ISP IPs changed nothing |
| + Advanced evasion | Beyond the tier this study holds |

A **control site rendered cleanly through every tier**, so the ladder works — the wall is the marketplace's, not our pipeline's. (The control is a public reference site that openly serves automated readers. A clean control says *we aren't broken* — not that the subject would render if the challenge lifted. That stays the open question a sanctioned re-run answers.)

One detail rules out the lazy explanation that *we* got flagged: switching to residential, home-ISP egress drew the **same challenge** — our egress IPs don't account for the block. That is one controlled swap in one window: enough to retire "they flagged our datacenter IPs," not enough to say what the challenge keys on.

### Where the ladder changed the read

The other wall only matters once an agent is *on* a page. Many modern storefronts assemble their product grid in the browser, after load — so a plain fetch reads the page title and almost no products, while render-aware timing reads the whole grid. On a comparable client-rendered storefront, that is the difference between **~7 products seen and ~28** ([the capability frontier](/how-it-works/capabilities) has the full demonstration). The subject was walled before we could render it, so this is the instrument's general capability, not a subject finding.

**The two walls are independent, and a page raises them in series.** To read a site's inventory an agent must be *reachable* (clear the challenge) **and** *readable* (render the grid); fail either and it comes back with less than the page holds. The marketplace stopped agents at the **first** wall this run, so the second never mattered. Most pages in the wild clear neither cleanly — which is why a study records *which* wall it hit, not just that the agent came up short.

## ③ What each trip wrote down — the data the report is built from

Every trip recorded the same three things as it ran — its transcript, its journey, and its access posture ([Anatomy of a Run](/evidence/anatomy-of-a-run) covers the shape in full):

<CardGroup cols={3}>
  <Card title="The journey" icon="route">
    For each trip, the sites it actually touched: the retailers and aggregators it reached and read, and the marketplace — recorded as access-limited where the challenge turned it away. Each source became one uniform **source card**, fetch or citation, the same currency across both lanes.
  </Card>
  <Card title="The declared winner" icon="trophy">
    Each trip's recommendation was taken as the **structured entity the agent itself named** — the retailer it steered the buyer toward — not a word guessed back out of its prose afterward. That is what a count is built from.
  </Card>
  <Card title="The access posture" icon="circle-half-stroke">
    **Reached** for the pages an agent read; **access-limited** for the marketplace pages the challenge closed. Counted as access-limited in the ledger — never silently dropped, never converted into a false fact about the site.
  </Card>
</CardGroup>

Before any "most agents" sentence exists, the ledger states how many trips were planned, completed, and metric-eligible — and, here, how many had the subject's pages access-limited ([the count ledger](/insights/reading-a-report/count-ledger)):

| Ledger line | This study |
|---|---|
| Trips planned | 6 |
| Completed | 6 |
| Metric-eligible | 6 |
| Subject's own pages access-limited in | 6 of 6 |

The subject was access-limited *within* otherwise-complete trips — every trip still reached other sources, answered, and counts — which is why access-limited reads 6 of 6 while all 6 stay metric-eligible. **Six is the denominator for everything below, and it is small on purpose** — a 25-trip panel is what tightens it from a signal into a rate.

## ④ From evidence to report — computed first, narrated second

The trips produced a distribution, and the report is built from it in a fixed order: **the verdict is computed before a sentence is written** ([The Honesty Layer](/evidence/honesty-layer)). Code first read the audited evidence and computed the thesis — which retailer each trip steered the buyer to, the tally, the verdict class, and a confidence band tied to how much evidence carried. On this study the computed object was unambiguous:

> Across the **6 metric-eligible trips**: the marketplace appeared in **all 6** answers but **led 0 of 6**; a national retailer the agents could read live **led 4 of 6**. With six trips, *led 0 of 6* is a **directional null** — *led none in this probe*, not a 0% rate. The honest read is "the marketplace didn't surface as the destination here," with the small denominator stated, not a population claim.

Only then did the language model **narrate** that computed object — it cannot author a headline the evidence doesn't support, such as the marketplace winning when the evidence made it a footnote. The **claim boundary** travelled onto the artifact as a required field: this is observed agent behavior under stated conditions — not market share, not demand, not a ranking of the sites.

One thing the verdict is careful **not** to claim: it can't separate *led 0 because blocked* from *led 0 on the merits*. A site an agent can't open can't win on its live inventory, so the access wall and the result are entangled — and a marketplace of dealer listings might simply fit this prompt worse than a buy-online retailer, block or no block. Whether it would lead **if an agent could read it** is the open question a sanctioned re-run answers. The report states that entanglement rather than narrating past it.

The headline that survived is narrow: *given what the agents could read this run, they recommended **around** the marketplace, not **through** it.* That is a finding about what the agents read — not a verdict on the marketplace's inventory, and the report states that on its face.

## ⑤ When a site turns the agents away — reported honestly

The most important finding here is the one easiest to get wrong. The tempting headline writes itself: *"AI agents can't reach this marketplace."* The evidence does not support it — it is exactly the claim the [honesty layer](/evidence/honesty-layer) is built to keep off the page.

Start with what the agents experienced. A browsing trip that tried to search the marketplace hit the challenge and said so itself:

> "I tried to run filtered searches directly on [the marketplace], but ran into a security block (403). This forced me to rely on the other sources."
>
> *— an agent, mid-task, verbatim*

The engine records that trip as **access-limited** and counts it as access-limited — the trip is not dropped, and the gap is not converted into "the marketplace has no good inventory." A reach gap is a property of the trip that hit it, never a fact about the site ([Two Lanes](/how-it-works/two-lanes#attribution-before-the-verdict-not-spin-after) owns this discipline).

Now the part that takes discipline. **"We couldn't reach it" is a statement about the testing tool and this run — not automatically about every agent.** The chain is *because → therefore*, and it stops where the evidence does:

<Steps>
  <Step title="The easy dismissals are already ruled out">
    The residential swap drew the same challenge, and the control site rendered clean through every tier (both above) — so it isn't our IPs, and it isn't a broken pipeline. What the challenge turns away is **automated** browsers.
  </Step>
  <Step title="A real shopper's own browser may clear it">
    A human-driven agent inside a person's logged-in browser carries the signals an interactive challenge is built to pass — real history, cookies, human interaction. What gets filtered is **automated, server-side** access, including agent-readiness tooling like ours and the crawlers that feed the search-native lane.
  </Step>
  <Step title="So the engine ships only the narrow claim">
    *Automated agent-readiness testing is challenged here without sanctioned access* — **not** "no agent can ever see this site."
  </Step>
</Steps>

<Note>
  There is even a **time signal** in the capture: our rendered tier read this same marketplace cleanly about a month earlier in the engagement, and meets the challenge now. Whether that is the site hardening or our automated fingerprint aging, it is recorded as what it is — an observation about reach on a date, never a trend dressed up as a fact.
</Note>

## What a read like this is for

A read like this isn't a scorecard to file — it's the start of three moves, each resolving to a **re-run on the same pinned instrument**. The before/after is the deliverable.

<Steps>
  <Step title="Decide the access on your terms">
    If automated agent-readiness traffic is challenged at your door, open a **sanctioned test lane** — an allowlisted IP range, a signed agent header, a bounded rate on public listing pages only. This measures the site without weakening the defense that turns away everyone else. Then re-run to see whether being readable changes where the site lands.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Make the page readable once an agent is in">
    The readability wall is independent of access: an agent that clears the challenge still reads a client-rendered grid as a near-empty page. Server-render the listing, or expose a structured product feed, and an agent that reaches the site reads its inventory. The re-run measures the lift.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Watch it as a standing metric, not a snapshot">
    Pin the panel and re-run on a cadence. Reached / access-limited and lead-share become a trendline — pre/post a site change, quarter over quarter — instead of a one-time read.
  </Step>
</Steps>

**Sizing whether it matters this quarter** is the one thing this study can't do alone. It reports where a site stands: where agents steer the buyer, and which sites they read to get there. What that's worth takes two inputs the customer holds — what share of the category's discovery is already agent-mediated, and what a buyer who arrives on an agent's recommendation converts at versus an organic one. Paired with the lead-share read, those form the business case; the instrument supplies the half not visible from a merchant's own logs.

  <Accordion title="You couldn't read the subject site — so what's the study even worth?">
    It measured the agent's **real choice set**: where leading agents steer a buyer in this category, which sites they read live, and where the subject landed. The access limit is itself a recorded finding — a property of the run and the tool, not "no agent can see the site." An aggressive challenge keeps automated agents from reading the subject's live inventory while competing pages read clean — that is the actionable result.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Did you really climb the whole ladder, or stop at the cheap tier and call it blocked?">
    Logged at every tier: plain fetch, rendered browser, and residential egress — the same challenge each time, including at the residential tier (which is how we know it isn't our egress IPs). The one tier above that is beyond this study's plan, and the page says so. A control site rendered clean through all of them.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Can a custom study turn the ladder all the way up?">
